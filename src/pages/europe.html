Cryptids of Europe

European Cryptids


Europe is home to a wide variety of mysterious beings rooted in folklore, history, and eyewitness accounts. From deep lake serpents to forest witches, each cryptid carries its own mythology and mystery. Explore the carousel below and click on each creature to learn more about their origins and sightings.

Lock Ness Monster

  • Country: Scotland
  • First Sighting: 565 AD
  • Type: Sea Monster

The Loch Ness Monster, affectionately nickenamed Nessie, is perhaps the most iconic figure in cryptozoology. Nessie has become a near-synonym for the field itself and has received more sustained media attention than almost any other cryptid, rivaled only by Bigfoot and perhaps beings like the so-called Grey Aliens.


Sightings commonly describe a creature about twenty feet long, with sleek, rubbery black-gray skin and a long, serpentine body lined with humps. It's often said to have paddle-like limbs (or short legs), and a head vaguely shaped like a horse's— sometimes with a shaggy mane, small horns, or a crest. A rounder, turtle-like head is also reported, and this is the version most often seen in famous photographs.

Kraken

  • Country: Norway
  • First Sighting: 13th Century
  • Type: Giant Cephalopod

The Kraken is a legendary sea monster said to dwell off the coast of Norway and Greenland. First described in Norse sagas and later chronicled in scientific texts of the 18th century, the Kraken was often depicted as a massive, tentacled beast capable of dragging entire ships beneath the waves. Its size was exaggerated in folklore, sometimes said to span over a mile in length, with arms that could wrap around a vessel.


Though commonly compared to a giant squid, the Kraken blends fact and fiction—possibly inspired by sightings of real deep-sea creatures like the colossal squid. For centuries, sailors feared it as a deadly menace of the deep, cementing its place in maritime myth and cryptozoology.

Lagarfljót Worm

  • Country: Iceland
  • Sighting: 1345
  • Type: Lake Serpant

The Lagarfljót Worm, or Lagarfljótsormurinn, is a legendary lake monster said to inhabit Lake Lagarfljót in eastern Iceland. First recorded in 1345, the creature is described as a massive, serpentine being, sometimes with humps or coils that rise above the water’s surface. According to folklore, it originated from a small "lingworm" placed on a gold ring to grow—believed to increase the ring’s value—which eventually grew so large and monstrous it had to be thrown into the lake. The worm is said to cause mysterious disturbances in the water and has occasionally been blamed for damage to boats and property.


Modern sightings continue to be reported, including a 2012 viral video that sparked renewed debate. Whether myth, misidentification, or something more, the Lagarfljót Worm remains one of Iceland's most enduring and enigmatic legends.

Baba Yaga

  • Country: Russia, and Slavic Regions
  • Sighting: 1755
  • Type: Witch

Baba Yaga is a legendary witch-like entity from Slavic folklore, often considered more than just a myth. Descriptions vary, but she is typically portrayed as an unnaturally old woman with iron teeth, sunken eyes, and a long hooked nose. Witnesses—both folkloric and anecdotal—describe her dwelling deep in the forest in a mobile hut that stands on giant chicken legs. She's said to fly in a mortar and steer with a pestle, often accompanied by strange winds and unsettling sounds.


Though many accounts depict her as hostile or predatory, others suggest she can act as a guardian of hidden knowledge or even help those who approach her with respect. While rooted in folk tales, Baba Yaga has continued to appear in modern reports, particularly in isolated rural areas, leading some to speculate about her status as a cryptid or supernatural entity.
